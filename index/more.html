The Southern Ocean drives the great circulation on the Earth

The Southern Ocean plays the central role for the global ocean circulation (called the meridional overturning circulation or MOC), from which the densest and largest water mass: Antarctic Bottom Water is produced. This is because seawater here tends to be extremely cooled. In the Southern Ocean, seawater actually not only sinks, but also upwells so as to close the circuit of MOC. MOC is crucial for the climate, especially in terms of heat and carbon transportation.

According to IPCC AR5, it is likely that the bottom layer of ocean warmed for the period 1992 to 2005, with the largest warming observed in the Southern Ocean. Though this change is amazingly drastic for the tranquil abyssal ocean, the cause still to be in the dark.

The ocean rapidly melts the Antarctic land ice

Cryosphere is defined as a region in which water of solid state (ice) exists. Ice is also essential for the global climate dynamics. The Southern Ocean is adjoining to the Antarctic Ice Sheet, which is a giant reservoir of fresh water as much as 60 m elevation of sea-level equivalent. Regarding ongoing trials to reduce uncertainity of prediction for sea-level rise, assessment of its dynamical responce to the ocean is of paramount importance.

Here the point is, intrusion of warm water from offshore onto the continental shelves. This heat supplying process not only melts ice shelves (floating front of ice sheet), but also affects MOC through modifying the property (temperature and salinity) of water on the shelf.

Subpolar gyre as an mediator between the ocean and ice shelves

Subpolar gyre in the Southern Ocean can be characterized by northern ACC (Antarctic Circumpolar Current, eastward) and southern ASC (Antarctic Slope Current, westward). This cyclonic (clockwise) circulation separates poleward cold water from warm water offshore. Hence, the dynamics of the subpolar gyre becomes the focus, considering the process of intruding warm water.
