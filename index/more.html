Why the Southern Ocean?

The Southern Ocean plays the substantial role for the global climate system, especially in producing the densest and largest watermass in the world: Antarctic Bottom Water, because seawater there tends to become extremely cold. The formation of bottom water is conceived as one of the dynamical driver of the Meridional Overturning Circulation (MOC). In the Southern Ocean, seawater does not only sink, but also upwell so as to close the circuit of MOC, just like a hub of the global ocean. The MOC's variability is important for the climate as a transporter of heat and carbon.

According to IPCC AR5, it is likely that the bottom layer of ocean warmed for the period 1992 to 2005, with the largest warming observed in the Southern Ocean. This change is so drastic for the tranquil abyssal ocean, however, this cause yet to be in the dark.

The rapid melt of the Antarctic land ice

Ice is also essential for the climate system. The Southern Ocean is adjoining to the Antarctic Ice Sheet, which is a giant reservoir of fresh water equivalent to 60 m elevation of sea surface height. Regarding ongoing trials to reduce the uncertainity of the prediction of sea-level rise, the dynamical responce of the Ice Sheets to the oceanic condition needs to be adressed.

The point is, intrusion of warm water offshore onto the continental shelves. This heat provision does melt the ice shelves (floating of ice sheet front), possibly leading to modification of the MOC through the dynamical conditioning, e.g., stratification and convection.

Subpolar gyre as a mediator between the ocean and ice shelves

Synoptic-scale circulation in the Southern Ocean can be characterized by the northern ACC (Antarctic Circumpolar Current) flowing eastward and the southern ASC (Antarctic Slope Current), flowing westward. They consist a cyclonic (clockwise) circulation separating the poleward cold water from the warm water offshore. Therefore, the subpolar gyre can collect our interests, regarding the intrusion process onto the shelves.
